# NRR Intelligence Assessment — Question Content (v2)

This is the single source of truth for the Retention, Expansion, and Pricing blocks.
NRR Reporting block is UNCHANGED and not covered here.

## Framework

- 3 capabilities: Revenue Retention, Revenue Expansion, Pricing Optimization
- 6 intelligence levers per capability: Impact, Whitespace, Accountability, Playbook, Execution, Governance
- Each question presents 5 unlabeled scenarios; user picks one
- Each scenario maps to an L-value 1–5
- That L-value IS the score for that capability × lever cell

No People/Process/Tech/Data dimensions. They are removed entirely.

## Scoring

- Cell score = L-value of scenario picked (1–5)
- Capability score = average of the 6 lever L-values for that capability (1.00–5.00)
- Overall maturity score = average of all 18 cells across the 3 redesigned capabilities
- Stage mapping:
  - 1.0–1.49 Reactive
  - 1.5–2.49 Diagnostic
  - 2.5–3.49 Operational
  - 3.5–4.49 Optimized
  - 4.5–5.0 Intelligent

# CAPABILITY 1: REVENUE RETENTION

## Q1 — Impact intelligence
**Question:** Can your team show, right now, the quantified value each customer is getting from you — or do you only know at QBR time, in slides, after the fact?

- L1 — We don't really measure this. If the customer is happy and using the product, we assume value is there.
- L2 — CSMs know it qualitatively for their top customers, from conversations. Nothing systematic.
- L3 — We capture impact at QBRs and renewals. It lives in slides — backward-looking, not continuous.
- L4 — Realized value is tracked per customer in a system. Reviewed monthly, used in renewal forecasting.
- L5 — Value signals are continuous and predictive. We know if a customer is silently losing faith before they do.

## Q2 — Whitespace intelligence
**Question:** If a customer was going to churn in the next two quarters, would your system tell you — or would you find out from the customer?

- L1 — We find out when the customer tells us. We don't have a system for spotting churn risk early.
- L2 — We catch it from soft signals — CSM gut, a missed meeting, a support pattern. Often too late.
- L3 — Quarterly risk review surfaces big risks. Smaller patterns slip through.
- L4 — Health scores and risk signals live in the CRM. The team reviews them weekly and escalates.
- L5 — Risk fires automatically from leading indicators across product, support, and stakeholder data. We act before the customer feels it.

## Q3 — Accountability intelligence
**Question:** Is retention something specific people in your org are measured, paid, and visibly accountable for — or is it everyone's job and no one's number?

- L1 — Retention isn't really in anyone's number. CS is "responsible" but nothing measurable hangs on it.
- L2 — CS comp is tied to satisfaction, tenure, or coverage. Retention is implicit, not explicit.
- L3 — CSMs have renewal targets, but they're soft. Most variable comp is elsewhere.
- L4 — CSMs and CS leaders carry GRR and NRR targets, with clear bonus tied to retention outcomes.
- L5 — Retention is weighted across CS, Sales, and Product. Comp, dashboards, and operating reviews are all aligned to net retention.

## Q4 — Playbook intelligence
**Question:** When a customer turns red, is there a real recovery plan with named owners and a timeline — or does it depend on whichever CSM happens to own them?

- L1 — Honestly, we don't have a defined recovery motion. We hope, or we discount.
- L2 — Depends on the CSM. They do what they can with what they have.
- L3 — We pull a recovery plan together for the biggest red customers. Quality is uneven across the team.
- L4 — Every red customer has a defined recovery plan — named owners, actions, review cadence.
- L5 — Recovery plans are templated, tracked with milestone status, and reviewed in operating cadence with learnings fed back into the system.

## Q5 — Execution intelligence
**Question:** When a retention risk fires — a red customer, a champion loss, a usage drop — how fast can your team actually mobilize and act on it?

- L1 — Slow. Weeks, or until something forces it to the top of the pile.
- L2 — It depends. Sometimes days, sometimes weeks. It moves when someone senior pushes.
- L3 — We respond, but the path from signal to action is manual. There's a meeting, then a follow-up, then someone acts.
- L4 — Signals route to defined owners with a target response window. Most fire within days, not weeks.
- L5 — Risk signals trigger named actions automatically, assigned and tracked. Response time is measured and improving.

## Q6 — Governance intelligence
**Question:** Is retention something you actively run in your operating rhythm — or does it only surface when something breaks?

- L1 — It only surfaces when something breaks — a big churn, a board question. No regular cadence.
- L2 — It comes up when there's noise, otherwise it's CS's problem.
- L3 — Quarterly retention review. Numbers get reported but action items don't always follow through.
- L4 — Monthly retention review with leadership. Risks, recoveries, and patterns tracked across the portfolio.
- L5 — Net retention is a top-line metric in every operating cadence, with clear owners, leading indicators, and learnings feeding back into how the team operates.

# CAPABILITY 2: REVENUE EXPANSION

## Q1 — Impact intelligence
**Question:** Before you walk into an expansion conversation, can you show the customer the quantified value they've already gotten — or are you asking them to buy more on faith?

- L1 — We don't really quantify it. We rely on the relationship and the customer's general sentiment.
- L2 — The rep or CSM puts together impact numbers when an expansion conversation comes up. It's anecdotal and uneven.
- L3 — We pull a value summary from QBR materials before an expansion ask. It's backward-looking and slide-based.
- L4 — Realized value per customer is tracked continuously and used to frame every expansion conversation.
- L5 — The value story is live, quantified, and tied to the specific expansion ask — we walk in with proof, not pitch.

## Q2 — Whitespace intelligence
**Question:** If I asked you right now which customers have the most untapped expansion potential — and where — could your team tell me, with data?

- L1 — Not really. We know our biggest customers but not where the headroom is across the base.
- L2 — The reps know their own accounts. There's no portfolio-level view of whitespace.
- L3 — We do whitespace exercises once or twice a year — usually for planning. The data goes stale fast.
- L4 — Whitespace is mapped per customer in our CRM — product gaps, seat gaps, tier gaps — and refreshed quarterly.
- L5 — Whitespace is live and signal-driven — we see opportunity emerge across the portfolio in real time.

## Q3 — Accountability intelligence
**Question:** Is expansion in a specific person's number — with comp, targets, and visibility — or does it just happen when it happens?

- L1 — It just happens. Nobody owns it as a number. We celebrate it when it comes in.
- L2 — Sales or CS will pursue expansion when they see it, but it's not formally in their plan.
- L3 — Account managers have expansion targets, but they're soft and most of the comp is elsewhere.
- L4 — AMs and CSMs carry explicit expansion targets with bonus tied to them. Quotas are set and tracked.
- L5 — Expansion is owned across Sales, CS, and Product with aligned comp, dashboards, and operating reviews.

## Q4 — Playbook intelligence
**Question:** When an expansion opportunity surfaces — usage spike, new buyer, new use case — is there a defined motion to pursue it, or does it depend entirely on the rep?

- L1 — It depends on the rep. The best ones run their own playbook, the rest improvise.
- L2 — We have some shared best practices, but they're tribal. Quality varies a lot.
- L3 — We have documented motions for the main expansion types. Adoption is uneven across the team.
- L4 — Defined plays exist for each expansion type, tied to triggers, and most reps run them consistently.
- L5 — Plays are triggered automatically by signal, assigned to the right owner, and measured for win rate.

## Q5 — Execution intelligence
**Question:** Once an expansion play kicks off, how reliably does it get driven to a decision — with the right people, the right timing, and the right asks — instead of stalling?

- L1 — Plays often stall. They lose momentum once the initial enthusiasm fades.
- L2 — It depends who's driving. Some reps push them through, others let them drift.
- L3 — We track active expansion plays and review them, but slippage is common in the middle stages.
- L4 — Every active play has a defined cadence, named owner, and clear next-step accountability. Slippage is visible and addressed.
- L5 — Plays are tracked end-to-end with stage-by-stage measurement. Bottlenecks surface fast and feed back into the motion.

## Q6 — Governance intelligence
**Question:** Is expansion something you actively run in your operating rhythm — or does it surface mainly at renewal time, as an afterthought?

- L1 — It surfaces at renewal, if at all. Otherwise we're focused on new logos and retention.
- L2 — Expansion gets reviewed in big-deal forecast calls but not as its own discipline.
- L3 — Quarterly expansion review. Pipeline gets discussed, action items follow inconsistently.
- L4 — Monthly expansion review with leadership. Pipeline, conversion, and patterns tracked across the portfolio.
- L5 — Expansion is a top-line operating metric — pipeline, win rate, leading indicators — with learnings fed back into plays.

# CAPABILITY 3: PRICING OPTIMIZATION

## Q1 — Impact intelligence
**Question:** Before you propose a price increase or new pricing structure to a customer, can you show them the quantified value they've gotten — or is the conversation mostly about cost and market rates?

- L1 — We don't anchor pricing to value delivered. Conversations default to market benchmarks and customer pushback.
- L2 — The rep or CSM puts together value numbers when a pricing conversation comes up. It's anecdotal.
- L3 — We pull value data from QBR materials before a renewal pricing conversation. Backward-looking, slide-based.
- L4 — Realized value per customer is tracked continuously and used to frame every pricing conversation.
- L5 — Pricing conversations open with a quantified value story tied to outcomes — we ask for more because we can prove more.

## Q2 — Whitespace intelligence
**Question:** If I asked you which customers are underpriced relative to the value they're getting — and by how much — could your team tell me, with data?

- L1 — Not really. We know list price vs. what we charge, but not value vs. price by customer.
- L2 — Reps have a gut feel for which customers are getting a deal. There's no portfolio-level view.
- L3 — We do pricing reviews once or twice a year and flag underpriced customers for renewal. Data goes stale fast.
- L4 — Underpricing is tracked per customer — value delivered vs. price paid — and refreshed quarterly. Reps see it before renewal.
- L5 — Value-to-price gap is live across the portfolio, signals fire when customers drift into underpriced territory.

## Q3 — Accountability intelligence
**Question:** Is pricing — price realization, discount discipline, renewal uplift — in a specific person's number? Or is it everyone's responsibility and no one's metric?

- L1 — Pricing isn't really in anyone's number. We track ARR but not price realization or discount erosion.
- L2 — Finance watches it, but it's not in Sales or CS comp. Pricing slippage is reported, not owned.
- L3 — Sales has a discount-cap policy but enforcement is uneven. Renewal uplift is a soft target.
- L4 — Sales and CS leaders carry explicit targets on price realization and renewal uplift, with comp tied to it.
- L5 — Pricing outcomes are owned across Sales, CS, RevOps, and Finance with aligned comp, dashboards, and operating reviews.

## Q4 — Playbook intelligence
**Question:** When a pricing event happens — renewal, expansion, partial churn, cross-sell — does the team have a defined motion to capture the right price, or does it depend on the rep?

- L1 — It depends entirely on the rep. The best ones negotiate well; the rest leave money on the table.
- L2 — We have some shared best practices and discount guidelines, but they're tribal and inconsistently applied.
- L3 — We have defined motions for renewal uplift and basic discounting. Packaging, bundling, and reinstatement are case-by-case.
- L4 — Defined playbooks cover renewal uplift, expansion pricing, bundle offers, and partial-churn repricing. Contracts have built-in protections (penalties, reinstatement, abuse-of-license).
- L5 — Pricing motions are systematic — predefined upsell paths, packaged bundles for consolidation, automatic repricing on product drop, and tested discount tiers — built into how every deal is structured.

## Q5 — Execution intelligence
**Question:** When a rep quotes a deal — renewal, expansion, restructure — how disciplined is the path from opportunity to signed contract? Is it driven by a system, or by spreadsheets and approvals up the chain?

- L1 — Quotes are spreadsheets. Discount approvals are negotiated up the management chain, deal by deal.
- L2 — We have a CPQ tool but it's underused. Most real quoting still happens off-system.
- L3 — CPQ handles most deals and is integrated with CRM. Discount guidance is rule-based but static — doesn't adapt to the deal.
- L4 — CPQ handles the vast majority of expansion and renewal quoting, with dynamic deal-scoring giving reps tailored discount guidance per deal.
- L5 — Quoting is fully automated with deal-scoring models that surface optimal pricing per deal and learn from outcomes — discount slippage is measured and shrinking.

## Q6 — Governance intelligence
**Question:** Is pricing run in your operating rhythm — price realization, discount trends, packaging performance reviewed in cadence — or is pricing set once and revisited rarely?

- L1 — Pricing is revisited when it breaks — a major deal, a competitor move, a board ask. No regular cadence.
- L2 — Pricing comes up in annual planning. Otherwise it's RevOps or Finance running the numbers in isolation.
- L3 — Quarterly pricing review. Discount trends and renewal uplift get reported, but action items don't always follow through.
- L4 — Monthly pricing review with leadership. Price realization, discount discipline, package performance tracked across the portfolio.
- L5 — Pricing is a top-line operating metric — realization, leakage, package mix, renewal uplift — with learnings feeding back into playbooks and contracts.
